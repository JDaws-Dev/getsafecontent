YouTube has over 800 million videos. My step-daughters—ages 12 and 5—seem determined to watch approximately 800 million of them.

As a new step-dad, I've spent way too many hours researching every parental control option out there. The challenge: finding the educational, entertaining, and enriching content while keeping them away from everything else.

Here's every parental control option YouTube offers in 2026—plus the third-party alternatives that fill the gaps. This is everything I wish I'd known when I started.

## Option 1: Restricted Mode

**What it is**: YouTube's built-in content filter

**How to enable**:
1. Open YouTube (web or app)
2. Tap your profile picture → Settings
3. Find "Restricted Mode" and toggle it on
4. On desktop, you can lock it with your Google account

**What it filters**:
- Mature content based on age-restrictions
- Videos flagged by users
- Content with mature themes

**Pros**:
- Free and built-in
- Works on any YouTube app
- Can be locked to prevent changes

**Cons**:
- Not comprehensive—content slips through
- Over-blocks some legitimate educational content
- Community-driven, so new content isn't always caught

**Best for**: A basic first layer of protection

## Option 2: Supervised Accounts

**What it is**: YouTube experience for kids under 13, managed through Google Family Link

**How to set up**:
1. Install Google Family Link on your phone
2. Create a Google account for your child
3. Open YouTube on their device
4. Choose their content level:
   - **Explore**: Ages 9+ (kid-focused content)
   - **Explore More**: Ages 13+ (more content, still filtered)
   - **Most of YouTube**: Adult supervision recommended

**What you can control**:
- Content level (the three options above)
- Search on/off
- Watch history visible to parents
- Time limits

**Pros**:
- More granular than Restricted Mode
- Persists across devices (tied to account)
- Parent visibility into what they're watching

**Cons**:
- Only for kids under 13
- Still algorithm-driven filtering
- Can't approve specific channels

**Best for**: Tweens who've outgrown YouTube Kids

## Option 3: YouTube Kids App

**What it is**: Separate app with curated, kid-focused content

**Who it's for**: Children 12 and under

**Content levels**:
- **Preschool**: Ages 4 and under
- **Younger**: Ages 5-8
- **Older**: Ages 9-12

**Features**:
- No comments
- Curated content (algorithmically)
- Timer controls
- Search can be disabled
- Parent-approved content only mode

**Pros**:
- Designed specifically for kids
- No comment section
- Separate from main YouTube

**Cons**:
- Disturbing content has slipped through filters
- Limited content (many channels not available)
- Kids often "outgrow" it and want real YouTube
- Algorithm still makes decisions, not you

**Best for**: Younger children (under 8) with close parental supervision

## Option 4: Block YouTube Entirely

**Methods**:
- Router-level DNS blocking
- Screen Time (iOS) app restrictions
- Third-party apps like Bark or Qustodio

**Pros**:
- Complete protection
- No ambiguity

**Cons**:
- Loses educational value
- Kids feel left out socially
- Pushes usage to friends' houses where you have no control

**Best for**: Very young children or temporary "detox" periods

## Option 5: Third-Party Monitoring

**Apps like Bark ($14/month)**:
- Monitors what kids watch
- Alerts parents to concerning content
- Works after the fact (reactive)

**Pros**:
- Visibility into viewing habits
- Catches concerning patterns

**Cons**:
- Doesn't prevent exposure—only reports it
- Some families uncomfortable with monitoring

**Best for**: Teens where blocking isn't practical

## Our Recommendation by Age

**Ages 4 and under**: YouTube Kids with search disabled. Watch together when possible.

**Ages 5-8**: YouTube Kids or Supervised Accounts. Check their watch history regularly.

**Ages 9-12**: Supervised Accounts work, but this is when content gets tricky. Consider approval-based for more control.

**Ages 13+**: Restricted Mode plus regular conversations about content. Or approval-based if you want tighter controls.

**Any age where you want certainty**: An approval-based approach is the only way to know exactly what's available.

## The Bottom Line

YouTube has more content than any human could ever review. That's both its power and its problem.

The built-in tools (Restricted Mode, Supervised Accounts, YouTube Kids) are all filtering-based—trying to block the bad stuff. They work imperfectly.

The alternative is approval-based: only allowing content you've said yes to. It's more work upfront but provides complete confidence.
