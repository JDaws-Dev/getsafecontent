When I became a step-dad, I inherited two daughters—ages 12 and 5—and both of them were already hooked on YouTube. The older one had outgrown YouTube Kids and wanted "real" YouTube. The younger one kept finding weird videos that slipped through the filters.

YouTube Kids was supposed to solve the "YouTube problem" for parents. But like many families, we discovered it's not quite the solution it promises to be.

Maybe you're in the same boat. Maybe your kids have outgrown YouTube Kids. Maybe you've seen inappropriate content slip through its filters. Maybe you just want your kids to use the real YouTube with channels you've actually vetted.

Whatever your reason, here's how to make regular YouTube safer for your kids—with or without YouTube Kids.

## Why Some Parents Skip YouTube Kids

Before we dive into solutions, let's acknowledge why you might be here:

- **Age limitations**: YouTube Kids is designed for 12 and under. What about your 13-year-old?
- **Filter failures**: Disturbing content has slipped through YouTube Kids' automated filters
- **Limited content**: Some educational channels aren't on YouTube Kids
- **Kids want "real" YouTube**: The separate app feels babyish to older kids

These are all valid reasons to look for alternatives.

## Option 1: YouTube's Restricted Mode

YouTube has a built-in filter called Restricted Mode.

**To enable it:**
1. Open YouTube
2. Click your profile icon → **Settings**
3. Scroll to **Restricted Mode** and toggle it on

**What it does:**
- Hides videos that may contain mature content
- Works at the browser/app level
- Can be locked with your Google account

**The catch:**
Restricted Mode is imperfect. It's community-powered, so videos can slip through. It also sometimes blocks legitimate educational content. It's a blunt instrument.

## Option 2: Supervised Accounts (Google Family Link)

Google offers supervised YouTube experiences for kids under 13.

**To set up:**
1. Create a Google account for your child through Family Link
2. In YouTube settings, choose their content level:
   - **Explore**: Ages 9+
   - **Explore More**: Ages 13+
   - **Most of YouTube**: With some restrictions

**What it offers:**
- Age-based content filtering
- Search on/off toggle
- Viewing history for parents

**The catch:**
It still relies on YouTube's algorithms to determine what's appropriate. And those algorithms aren't always right.

## Option 3: Block YouTube Entirely

Some families take the nuclear option: block YouTube completely.

**Methods:**
- Router-level blocking (blocks all devices)
- Screen Time app limits (iOS)
- Third-party apps like Bark or Qustodio

**When this works:**
- Very young children
- Families who prefer no video streaming
- Temporary "detox" periods

**The catch:**
YouTube has genuine educational value. Blocking it entirely means missing channels like Crash Course, National Geographic Kids, and countless learning resources.

Instead of:
- Trusting YouTube's algorithm (risky)

## Our Recommendation

**For kids under 8**: YouTube Kids with search disabled is reasonable, with parental spot-checking.

**For kids 8-12**: Supervised accounts work, but consider approval-based if you want more control.

**For teens**: Restricted Mode plus ongoing conversations about content. Or SafeTube if you want tighter control without blocking YouTube entirely.

**For any age where you want complete confidence**: An approval-based approach like SafeTube is the only way to know exactly what's available.

## The Bottom Line

YouTube isn't inherently bad. It's an incredible educational resource. The problem is everything else mixed in with the good stuff.

The question isn't whether to let your kids use YouTube. It's how to let them access the good parts while protecting them from the rest.
