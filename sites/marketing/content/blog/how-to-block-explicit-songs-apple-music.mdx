When I became a step-dad to my 12-year-old, one of the first things I noticed was how much she loved music. Headphones on constantly. Apple Music playing nonstop.

And then I heard what was coming through those headphones.

If you're a parent who uses Apple Music, you've probably had the same moment: *How do I make sure my kids aren't listening to songs I'd never approve?*

Apple Music has over 100 million songs, and a good chunk of them contain lyrics that you probably don't want your kids hearing. The good news? Apple does offer some built-in parental controls. The bad news? They have significant limitations that I discovered the hard way.

Let me walk you through everything you need to know.

## Apple's Built-In Parental Controls

Apple provides a content restrictions feature through Screen Time. Here's how to set it up:

### On iPhone/iPad:
1. Go to **Settings** → **Screen Time**
2. Tap **Content & Privacy Restrictions**
3. Turn on **Content & Privacy Restrictions**
4. Tap **Content Restrictions** → **Music, Podcasts, News & Workouts**
5. Select **Clean**

### On Mac:
1. Open **System Settings** → **Screen Time**
2. Click **Content & Privacy**
3. Turn on **Content & Privacy**
4. Click **Store Restrictions**
5. Set Music to **Clean**

This tells Apple Music to hide songs marked as "Explicit" in the Apple Music catalog.

## The Problem with Apple's Approach

Here's what Apple doesn't tell you upfront:

**1. It relies on record labels being honest**

Apple's explicit tags come from the record labels themselves. If a label doesn't mark a song as explicit, it won't be filtered—even if the lyrics are inappropriate.

**2. "Clean" versions aren't always clean**

Many "clean" versions of songs just bleep out certain words. The themes, innuendo, and context remain. A song about topics you'd rather not explain to your 7-year-old doesn't become kid-friendly just because one word is muted.

**3. It's all or nothing**

You either block ALL explicit content or allow ALL of it. There's no way to say "I trust Taylor Swift but not this other artist." Every family is different, and a blanket filter doesn't account for that.

**4. Kids are resourceful**

If your child knows the passcode (or figures it out), they can disable restrictions. And if they're using a friend's device? Your settings don't apply.
